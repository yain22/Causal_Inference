Mathematical formulation

Outcome regression is a plug-in g-computation estimator: estimate the conditional outcome surface, evaluate that surface twice for every subject, and integrate over the empirical target-population distribution of X.

Graduate-level notation
Outcome-regression estimation map
Fit  mˆ(t,x) ⟶ {(mˆ(1,Xi), mˆ(0,Xi)) :i=1,…,n} ⟶ RDˆOR = 1n ∑i=1n { mˆ(1,Xi) − mˆ(0,Xi) }
1Fit the conditional mean
mt(x)≡E(Y|T=t,X=x)

Estimate this regression from the observed outcomes and treatment assignments.

2Predict both treatment worlds
mˆ1i=mˆ(1,Xi), mˆ0i=mˆ(0,Xi)

The same subject-specific covariate vector Xi appears in both predictions.

3Standardize and contrast
ψˆtOR =1n ∑i=1n mˆ(t,Xi)

For the ATE risk difference, subtract the standardized mean under t=0 from that under t=1.

Consistency

Y=Y(T)=TY(1)+(1−T)Y(0)

The observed outcome equals the potential outcome under the treatment actually received.

Conditional exchangeability

{Y(0),Y(1)}⟂T|X

All common causes of smoking and CVD required for adjustment are measured in X.

Positivity

0<P(T=1|X=x)<1

Each relevant covariate pattern has support in both treatment groups.

Identification by the g-formula
ψt≡E{Y(t)} =E[E{Y(t)|X}] =E[E{Y|T=t,X}] =E{mt(X)}
Important: the regression function is identified from observed data, but its interpretation as a causal potential-outcome mean requires all three assumptions above. Good predictive performance alone does not establish exchangeability.

Binary-outcome working model

logit{mβ(t,x)} =ηβ(t,x)
mβ(t,x)=expit{ηβ(t,x)}
Currently selected specification:

The model is fitted only once to observed Y. Counterfactual predictions are then generated by substituting t=1 and t=0 while keeping x fixed.

Why the treatment coefficient is not the ATE

βT≠E{Y(1)−Y(0)}

In logistic regression, βT is a conditional log-odds contrast. Non-collapsibility and treatment interactions prevent it from being interpreted as the marginal causal risk difference.

Model misspecification: if m̂(t,x) converges to an incorrect working surface m*(t,x), the plug-in estimator converges to E[m*(1,X) − m*(0,X)], which need not equal the causal ATE.

IFˆi,t = mˆ(t,Xi)−ψˆtOR +dˆtTMˆ−1Si(βˆ)

The first term captures variation in the empirical covariate distribution; the second propagates uncertainty from fitting β.

Wald standard error and 95% confidence interval
SEˆ(RDˆOR) =Var(IFˆi,RD)n , CI95%=RDˆOR±1.96SEˆ
Interpretation: this interval quantifies sampling uncertainty around the fitted plug-in estimator. It does not protect against unmeasured confounding or systematic outcome-model misspecification.
